Topic 5: Plants

1. Characteristics of the plant kingdom

Plants are eukaryotic organisms, meaning that their cells have a nucleus, and they are multicellular.

Plant cells have chloroplasts, a cell wall and large vacuoles. Plants are autotrophic organisms: they make their own food through photosynthesis.

Plants do not move from place to place, but they can make certain movements. Tropisms are growth movements in the direction of a stimulus. Nastic movements are non-permanent movements.

Plants can grow throughout their whole life. According to their size, they are classified as herbs, shrubs and trees.

2. Plant nutrition and photosynthesis

Plants only need inorganic substances to make their own food. This process is called photosynthesis.

During photosynthesis, light is captured by chlorophyll, a green substance found inside the chloroplasts of cells in leaves and stems.

Water + carbon dioxide + sunlight -> oxygen + organic substances

The roots absorb water and mineral salts. Carbon dioxide enters through the stomata, which regulate gas exchange and transpiration.

Respiration takes place in the mitochondria. In respiration, oxygen is consumed and carbon dioxide and water are released.

3. Transport of nutrients

More complex plants have conducting tissues. Xylem transports raw sap from the roots to the leaves. Raw sap is made up of water and mineral salts.

Phloem carries elaborated sap, made up of organic matter and water, from the leaves to the rest of the plant.

4. Plants without flowers

Mosses were the first land plants. They do not have conducting tissues, such as xylem and phloem. They live in humid environments because they absorb water through their whole surface. They do not have flowers or seeds and reproduce by spores.

In mosses, the gametophyte produces male and female gametes. With the help of water, fertilisation forms a zygote. The zygote grows into a sporophyte, and the sporophyte produces spores.

Ferns have conducting tissues: xylem and phloem. They live in forests and shady areas. They have an epidermis that prevents drying out. They do not have flowers, seeds or fruits, and they reproduce by spores produced in sori.

5. Plants with flowers

Vegetative organs are related to nutrition and support. Leaves carry out photosynthesis and transpiration. The stem supports the aerial part of the plant. The root fixes the plant to the soil, absorbs water and mineral salts, and stores reserve substances.

The flower is the reproductive organ of flowering plants. The stamens are the male reproductive organs. The pistil is the female reproductive organ.

Sexual reproduction has five stages: pollination, fertilisation, seed formation, fruit formation and germination.

In pollination, pollen moves from the stamens to the stigma. In fertilisation, the pollen tube reaches the ovary and joins with the ovule, forming the zygote. The fertilised ovule becomes the seed. The ovary becomes the fruit. During germination, the seed forms a new plant.

6. Asexual reproduction

Asexual reproduction takes place without the union of gametes. Stolons are aerial creeping stems that form new plants where they touch the soil. Rhizomes are horizontal underground stems. Bulbs are underground nutrient-storage organs formed by leaves around a bud. Tubers are thick underground stems or roots that store nutrients.

Artificial asexual reproduction includes cuttings and grafting. A cutting is a fragment of a plant used to produce a new plant. Grafting joins two compatible plants: one provides the roots and the other provides the aerial part.

7. Classification of flowering plants

Gymnosperms are the oldest flowering plants. Their flowers are not very showy and are grouped in cones. Male cones produce pollen. Female cones, or pine cones, have scales where the ovules are located.

Angiosperms are more evolved. They have showy flowers and fruits that protect the seeds and help seed dispersal.

Monocotyledons have one cotyledon, parallel veins, no main root axis, and flower parts in threes. Dicotyledons have two cotyledons, reticulate veins, a main root with branches, and flower parts in fours or fives.

Topic 6: Ecosystems

1. What is an ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a natural system made up of a group of living organisms, known as the biocenosis, which interact with one another and with the physical environment, known as the biotope.

Ecosystem = biocenosis + biotope + interactions

2. Food chains and food webs

Within an ecosystem, living organisms are classified according to the way they obtain food into producers, consumers and decomposers.

Producer organisms are autotrophic organisms that produce organic matter from inorganic substances. They include plants, algae and some bacteria.

Consumer organisms are heterotrophic organisms that feed on other organisms. They may be primary consumers or herbivores, secondary consumers or carnivores, and tertiary consumers or super-predators.

Decomposer organisms feed on organic remains, such as corpses and excrement. They include some bacteria, fungi and some animals.

A food chain represents who eats whom in an ecosystem. A food web represents all the food chains in an ecosystem.

3. Relationships between living organisms

Intraspecific relationships occur between individuals of the same species. They include family relationships, social relationships, competition and gregarious relationships.

Interspecific relationships occur between individuals of different species. They include parasitism, symbiosis, predation and mutualism.

4. Soil formation

Soil is a thin layer made up of mineral particles, organic matter, water, air and living organisms.

The process of soil formation is very slow and can take hundreds or thousands of years. It begins with the alteration of the parent rock. Later, small plants become established, fragments accumulate, and a deep soil forms.

Horizon A is the most superficial layer. Horizon B is the intermediate layer. Horizon C is the lower layer, formed by alteration of the parent rock.

5. Terrestrial biomes

The large ecosystems of the Earth are called biomes. Terrestrial biomes include the polar biome, tundra, taiga, deciduous forest, Mediterranean forest, desert, savanna and rainforest.

The polar biome is located at the poles and has low biodiversity. The tundra has frozen soil and scarce biodiversity. The taiga has coniferous forests. Deciduous forests and Mediterranean forests have abundant biodiversity. Deserts have very scarce biodiversity. Savannas have many herbivores and carnivores. Rainforests have enormous biodiversity.

6. Aquatic biomes

The coastal zone is found near the shore. Coral reefs are areas of very high biodiversity. The neritic zone is located on the continental shelf. The oceanic zone is located beyond the continental slope.

Most oceanic living beings are found in the photic zone, where light reaches. The abyssal zone is deep, has no light, and receives organic matter from waste that falls from above.
